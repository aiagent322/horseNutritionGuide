Trace Mineral

Zinc

A trace mineral essential for hoof horn synthesis, skin integrity, immune function, and enzyme activity — closely linked to copper in its dietary balance requirements.

Plain-English Summary

Zinc is required by more enzymes than any other trace mineral — over 300 zinc-dependent enzymes have been identified in mammals. In horses, zinc's most visible effects are on hoof quality, skin health, and coat condition. Zinc is a structural component of the keratin proteins that make up the hoof wall, skin, and hair. It is also essential for immune function, wound healing, and reproductive health. The dietary relationship between zinc and copper is critically important — these two minerals compete for absorption, and their ratio in the diet affects how well each is utilized. The recommended zinc-to-copper ratio is approximately 3–4 parts zinc to 1 part copper. Excess zinc suppresses copper absorption; excess copper suppresses zinc absorption. Getting both right requires evaluating them together.

What Zinc Does in the Horse's Body

Zinc serves three broad categories of function in the horse's body:

Catalytic: Zinc is a required cofactor for over 300 enzymes, including carbonic anhydrase (carbon dioxide transport), alkaline phosphatase (bone metabolism), and numerous proteases and peptidases. This catalytic role makes zinc essential for almost every major metabolic pathway in the body.

Structural: Zinc is a structural component of certain proteins, stabilizing their three-dimensional shape. Most notably, zinc stabilizes "zinc finger" protein domains involved in DNA binding and gene expression regulation. Zinc is also a structural component of Cu-Zn superoxide dismutase (the antioxidant enzyme shared with copper) and of insulin.

Regulatory: Zinc ions act as signaling molecules in immune cell function, influencing the activity of T and B lymphocytes and the production of inflammatory cytokines.

How Zinc Affects Your Horse

Hoof horn quality: Zinc is required for the normal synthesis and cross-linking of keratin proteins in the hoof wall, sole, and frog. Adequate zinc helps produce harder, more tightly-packed hoof horn with better structural integrity. Zinc deficiency contributes to soft, crumbly hoof horn, increased susceptibility to white line disease, and slower hoof growth. Zinc is most effective for hoof quality when copper and biotin are also adequate — these three nutrients work together in hoof horn synthesis.

Skin and wound healing: Zinc is essential for the proliferation of keratinocytes (skin cells) and fibroblasts (connective tissue cells) during wound healing. Zinc deficiency impairs wound closure and may cause skin lesions, particularly around the face, lower limbs, and areas subject to mechanical stress. Horses with poor wound healing or chronic skin conditions are often found to have suboptimal zinc status.

Immune function: Zinc deficiency impairs multiple aspects of immune function — T-lymphocyte development and function, natural killer cell activity, and antibody production are all zinc-dependent. Horses with chronically inadequate zinc may show increased susceptibility to infections and impaired response to vaccination.

Growth and reproduction: Zinc is required for normal cell division, making it particularly important during periods of rapid growth. Growing foals with zinc deficiency may show reduced growth rates, skeletal abnormalities, and impaired immune development. In breeding animals, zinc deficiency is associated with reduced reproductive performance.

The zinc-copper relationship: Zinc and copper compete for the same intestinal transporters (primarily divalent metal transporter-1, DMT-1). When one is present in excess relative to the other, it displaces the other from the transporter, reducing its absorption. This competitive relationship means that the ratio of zinc to copper in the diet is as important as the absolute amounts of each. The generally recommended Zn:Cu ratio for horses is 3–4:1. Ratios above 6:1 may begin to suppress copper absorption; ratios below 2:1 may suppress zinc absorption and favor copper. When evaluating a feed's mineral profile, always look at both zinc and copper values together and calculate their ratio.

Organic vs inorganic zinc: Zinc sulfate is the most common inorganic zinc form in horse feeds. Zinc proteinate, zinc amino acid chelate, and zinc methionine are organic forms that have demonstrated improved bioavailability in some research, particularly in the presence of dietary antagonists such as high iron, calcium, or phytate. Feeds listing zinc amino acid chelate or zinc proteinate use higher-bioavailability forms that may provide better tissue zinc status at the same dietary concentration.

Reading Zinc on a Feed Label

Zinc appears on the guaranteed analysis in mg/kg or ppm (minimum). The NRC (2007) recommends approximately 40 mg/kg dry matter for mature horses at maintenance, with higher requirements for growth. Evaluate zinc alongside copper — calculate the Zn:Cu ratio and ensure it falls in the 3–4:1 range for most horses.
