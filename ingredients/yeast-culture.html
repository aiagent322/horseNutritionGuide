Digestive Support — Prebiotic

Yeast Culture

A fermentation byproduct that supports hindgut microbial health and fiber digestibility — one of the most research-supported digestive ingredients in commercial horse feeds.

Plain-English Summary

Yeast culture — most commonly Saccharomyces cerevisiae yeast culture — is produced by growing yeast on a growth medium, then drying the whole fermentation culture including the yeast cells, the growth medium, and the metabolic byproducts of fermentation. It is not live yeast — it is a dried fermentation product that functions as a prebiotic, supporting the existing microbial population of the hindgut rather than introducing new organisms. Research in horses has shown that yeast culture supplementation can improve fiber digestibility, stabilize hindgut pH, and support microbial diversity. It is one of the most widely used and best-researched digestive support ingredients in commercial horse nutrition, and its effectiveness is not significantly compromised by pelleting heat, unlike live probiotic bacteria.

What Yeast Culture Is

Yeast culture is produced through a controlled fermentation process in which Saccharomyces cerevisiae yeast is grown on a substrate (growth medium), typically grain-based. The yeast ferments the substrate, producing metabolic byproducts including organic acids, enzymes, vitamins, and complex carbohydrates. The entire fermentation mass — yeast cells, substrate, and metabolic products — is then dried. The resulting product is not live yeast (dried yeast cells are not viable and do not multiply in the digestive tract) but contains a complex mixture of compounds produced during fermentation.

On labels: yeast culture, dried Saccharomyces cerevisiae fermentation product, Saccharomyces cerevisiae yeast culture, dried yeast. Live yeast preparations are distinct and may appear as active dry yeast or live yeast culture.

How Yeast Culture Affects Your Horse

Hindgut pH stability: One of the most consistent findings in equine yeast culture research is an improvement in hindgut pH stability. The cecum and large colon of the horse normally maintain a slightly acidic but relatively stable pH. When large amounts of starch reach the hindgut from grain overload, rapid fermentation produces lactic acid, dropping pH significantly — a condition called hindgut acidosis. Yeast culture appears to support the populations of fiber-fermenting bacteria (primarily cellulolytic bacteria) that compete with lactic acid producers, helping maintain a more stable pH environment.

Fiber digestibility: Multiple studies in horses have found that yeast culture supplementation improves the digestibility of fiber — the horse extracts more energy from the same amount of hay or forage when yeast culture is included in the diet. The proposed mechanism is that yeast culture metabolites stimulate the growth and activity of cellulolytic bacteria in the hindgut, improving their capacity to break down cellulose and hemicellulose. The practical implication is that horses on yeast culture may maintain body condition slightly better on the same amount of forage than unsupplemented horses.

Microbial diversity: A diverse hindgut microbial community is generally associated with better digestive health and resilience to disruption. Yeast culture has been associated with increased microbial diversity in some equine studies, potentially contributing to more robust digestive function.

Immune function: The cell wall of Saccharomyces cerevisiae contains beta-glucans and mannan oligosaccharides (MOS) — complex carbohydrates with documented immunomodulatory properties. MOS in particular binds to certain pathogens and may interfere with their attachment to the gut wall, providing a modest protective effect. These cell wall components survive the drying process and are present in yeast culture products.

Heat stability advantage: Unlike live probiotic bacteria, which can be substantially reduced or eliminated by the heat of pelleting and extrusion, yeast culture — being a dried fermentation product rather than live organisms — retains its activity through feed manufacturing processes. This makes yeast culture a reliable choice for pelleted and extruded feeds where live probiotic viability is a concern.

Stress and transition periods: The hindgut microbial population is particularly vulnerable to disruption during stress — transportation, competition, dietary changes, antibiotic treatment, and illness can all negatively affect the microbial community. Yeast culture is most frequently used in contexts where digestive stability is a priority: feeds for horses in training, horses traveling frequently, horses recovering from illness, and horses undergoing dietary transitions.

Cautions and Considerations

Yeast culture is safe and well-tolerated at normal feeding rates. It has no significant adverse effects documented at typical inclusion levels in commercial horse feeds. It is appropriate for horses of all ages and is commonly included in senior feeds, performance feeds, and feeds for horses prone to digestive upset. For horses with very severe digestive disease, yeast culture alone is not a substitute for veterinary treatment — it is a supportive supplement, not a medical intervention.
