Mineral — Calcium & Phosphorus Source

Dicalcium Phosphate

A mineral supplement providing both calcium and phosphorus in a 1.3:1 ratio — used in feeds to fine-tune the calcium-phosphorus balance alongside limestone.

Plain-English Summary

Dicalcium phosphate (DCP) is a mineral compound that provides both calcium and phosphorus simultaneously. Unlike limestone (which provides only calcium) or phosphate sources (which provide only phosphorus), DCP provides both in a naturally balanced ratio of approximately 1.3 parts calcium to 1 part phosphorus. Feed manufacturers use dicalcium phosphate alongside limestone to achieve precisely targeted calcium and phosphorus levels in finished feeds, fine-tuning the balance after accounting for what all other ingredients contribute. For the horse owner, the presence of dicalcium phosphate in a feed's ingredient list is a signal that the manufacturer has carefully considered mineral balance — it is a formulation tool, not a concern ingredient.

What Dicalcium Phosphate Is

Dicalcium phosphate (CaHPO₄) is an inorganic mineral compound — a calcium salt of phosphoric acid. Feed-grade DCP is approximately 18–21% calcium and 18–21% phosphorus. Its calcium-to-phosphorus ratio of approximately 1.3:1 is somewhat close to the ratio recommended for horses (1.5–2:1), making it useful for simultaneous supplementation of both minerals without dramatically skewing the ratio in either direction.

DCP is highly bioavailable — both the calcium and phosphorus are in forms that the horse's small intestine can absorb efficiently. On labels: dicalcium phosphate, deflourinated phosphate, monocalcium phosphate (a related compound with a different Ca:P ratio).

How Dicalcium Phosphate Affects Your Horse

The effects of dicalcium phosphate on the horse are the combined effects of its calcium and phosphorus contributions — described in detail in the limestone (calcium) and phosphorus sections of this guide. In the context of a commercially formulated feed, DCP itself does not produce distinct visible effects on the horse — its role is to ensure the feed provides adequate and appropriately balanced calcium and phosphorus, the effects of which are seen in bone strength, muscle function, and overall mineral status.

Phosphorus in horse nutrition: While calcium gets more attention, phosphorus is equally essential. Phosphorus is required for bone mineralization (as part of hydroxyapatite alongside calcium), for ATP (adenosine triphosphate) — the molecule that provides energy for every cellular process in the body — and for cell membrane structure (phospholipids). Phosphorus deficiency, though less common than calcium deficiency, causes bone abnormalities and impaired energy metabolism.

The balance in practice: Grain-based concentrate ingredients are generally higher in phosphorus than calcium. Pure grain diets have inverted Ca:P ratios. Feed manufacturers use limestone (high calcium, no phosphorus) and dicalcium phosphate (moderate both) in combination to achieve the target calcium and phosphorus levels while maintaining the appropriate ratio. Seeing both on the label means the manufacturer has balanced both minerals intentionally.

Cautions

Dicalcium phosphate is a well-established, safe feed ingredient at normal inclusion levels. The key concern with phosphorus in the total diet is the Ca:P ratio — evaluate this in the context of the complete diet including hay, not just the concentrated feed. Horses with chronic kidney disease may need phosphorus restriction; consult your veterinarian.
