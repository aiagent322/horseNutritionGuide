Sugar / Palatability — Metabolic Caution

Molasses

A byproduct of sugar refining widely used for palatability — but a significant sugar source that is not appropriate for all horses.

Plain-English Summary

Molasses is the thick, dark syrup left over after sugar crystals are extracted from sugarcane or sugar beet juice during refining. It is added to horse feeds primarily because horses find it highly palatable — it makes feed more attractive, reduces dust, and helps bind ingredients in textured feeds. However, molasses is genuinely high in sugar, and its position in the ingredient list tells you a great deal about how much of it is present. For horses with insulin resistance, equine metabolic syndrome, laminitis history, Cushing's disease, or PSSM, molasses is a significant concern and in many cases makes a feed inappropriate for that horse. For healthy horses in regular work, small amounts of molasses in a balanced feed are generally not problematic.

What Molasses Is

Molasses is produced as a byproduct of the sugar refining process. When sugarcane or sugar beets are processed, juice is extracted and boiled to crystallize the sucrose. Each boiling cycle — called a "strike" — extracts more sugar crystals and leaves behind a darker, more mineral-rich syrup. The final byproduct after maximum sugar extraction is blackstrap molasses, the darkest and most mineral-rich form. What reaches the horse feed industry is typically a blend of molasses grades, often called "cane molasses" or "beet molasses" depending on the source.

Molasses composition varies, but it typically contains approximately 45–55% total sugars (primarily sucrose, glucose, and fructose), along with moderate amounts of potassium, calcium, iron, and certain B vitamins. It also contains significant quantities of betaine, a compound that serves as a methyl donor in metabolism.

On horse feed labels, molasses may appear as: cane molasses, beet molasses, molasses products, or simply molasses. It can also be used as a coating on other ingredients — particularly beet pulp — in which case it may appear as "beet pulp with molasses" rather than as a standalone ingredient.

How Molasses Affects Your Horse

On blood glucose and insulin: The sugars in molasses — sucrose, glucose, and fructose — are rapidly digested and absorbed in the small intestine, causing a relatively rapid rise in blood glucose and a corresponding insulin response. For a metabolically healthy horse in regular work, this response is normal and managed well by the body. For horses with insulin resistance (IR), equine metabolic syndrome (EMS), or PPID/Cushing's disease, this insulin response is impaired — insulin levels spike higher and remain elevated longer than in a healthy horse. Chronically elevated insulin is directly associated with lamellar damage in the hoof and is the primary mechanism by which laminitis develops in metabolic horses.

This is why molasses is specifically contraindicated in feeds intended for horses with IR, EMS, laminitis history, or Cushing's disease. It is not merely a preference — it is a genuine physiological risk.

On palatability: Horses have a strong preference for sweet tastes. Molasses exploits this preference and is one of the most effective palatability enhancers used in horse feed. Feeds with molasses are typically eaten more readily, particularly by horses that are finicky eaters, horses that have recently experienced illness, and horses that have not been exposed to concentrate feeds. This palatability benefit is real and has legitimate uses — it can make the difference between a horse eating a necessary medication or supplement and refusing it.

On dust reduction and binding: Molasses acts as a natural binding agent and dust suppressant in textured feeds and pelleted products. The sticky syrup coats grain particles, reducing the fine dust that can irritate the respiratory tract. For horses with heaves (equine asthma) or other respiratory sensitivities, feeds with molasses may produce less respiratory irritation than dry, dusty feeds — though this benefit must be weighed against the sugar content.

On energy: Molasses contributes calories primarily as rapidly available sugar energy. This can be useful for horses that need a quick energy boost, but it is not the sustained, stable energy that performance horses benefit from most. Horses requiring significant caloric intake are better served by fat- and fiber-based energy sources that provide more consistent energy delivery without the blood sugar fluctuation.

On the hindgut: When molasses is present in large amounts, the rapidly fermentable sugars can pass into the hindgut if small intestinal capacity is exceeded, contributing to hindgut acidosis. This is more of a concern with very high molasses intake than with the trace amounts present in most commercial feeds.

Why Ingredient Position Matters

Commercial horse feed labels in the United States must list ingredients in descending order by weight — the first ingredient is present in the largest amount, and each subsequent ingredient is present in a smaller amount than the one before it.

For molasses, this means that its position in the ingredient list gives you meaningful information about how much is present:

  • Positions 1–3: Molasses is one of the largest components by weight. This is unusual and indicates a very high sugar content. Uncommon in modern feeds but occasionally seen in older-style sweet feeds.
  • Positions 3–6: Molasses is a meaningful contributor to the sugar content of the feed. This is common in traditional textured sweet feeds (oats, corn, barley with molasses). These feeds are not appropriate for metabolic horses.
  • Positions 7–12: Molasses is present in moderate amounts, likely used primarily for palatability and dust control. Still contributes to sugar and NSC content but less dominantly.
  • Position 13 or later: Molasses is a minor ingredient, present in small amounts primarily as a palatability agent or binder. Sugar contribution is modest but not zero.

This position-based interpretation is a rough guide, not a precise measurement. The actual sugar percentage is best determined by asking the manufacturer for the water-soluble carbohydrate (WSC) or NSC value of the finished feed.

Reading Molasses on a Feed Label

When molasses appears in a feed, look for the NSC, sugar (WSC), or starch values on the guaranteed analysis panel. If these are not listed, contact the manufacturer — this information is essential for horses with any metabolic concern.

For metabolic horses, most equine nutritionists recommend feeds with a total NSC of 10% or less. Feeds with molasses in the first five ingredients are very unlikely to meet this threshold.

The distinction between molasses as a standalone ingredient and molasses as a beet pulp coating is important. "Beet pulp with molasses" lists molasses as applied to the beet pulp, while in some feeds both "beet pulp" and "molasses" appear as separate ingredients. In the former case, the molasses is integrated into a single ingredient; in the latter, the molasses contribution can be evaluated independently by its position in the list.

Which Horses Should Avoid Molasses

  • Horses with insulin resistance (IR) or EMS — the insulin-stimulating effect of molasses sugar directly worsens these conditions
  • Horses with laminitis history — particularly relevant during recovery and for prevention of recurrence
  • Horses with PPID/Cushing's disease — impaired glucose regulation makes sugar-rich feeds inappropriate
  • Horses with PSSM type 1 or 2 — these horses require very low NSC diets; molasses significantly raises NSC
  • Easy keepers at risk of obesity — extra sugar calories are not beneficial

Which Horses Can Tolerate Molasses

  • Metabolically healthy horses in regular work — small to moderate amounts of molasses in a balanced feed are generally well-tolerated
  • Hard keepers needing caloric density — molasses adds calories, though fat and fiber are more efficient calorie sources
  • Horses that are picky eaters — the palatability benefit may justify the sugar content when a horse must eat a specific feed or medication
  • Young, growing horses in work — generally have robust insulin sensitivity and can handle moderate sugar intake
