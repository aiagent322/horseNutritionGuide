Limestone (Calcium Carbonate)

The most commonly used calcium supplement in commercial horse feeds — essential for bone, muscle, and nerve function, with a critical relationship to phosphorus that every horse owner should understand.

Plain-English Summary

Limestone is ground calcium carbonate (CaCO₃), the most common and economical calcium source used in commercial horse feeds. Calcium is the most abundant mineral in the horse's body — approximately 99% of it is stored in the skeleton, with the remaining 1% serving critical roles in muscle contraction, nerve transmission, blood clotting, and enzyme function. Limestone appears in horse feeds because most grain-based concentrate ingredients are low in calcium, and the feed must be supplemented to meet the horse's calcium requirement. The calcium content of a feed must always be considered in relation to phosphorus — the calcium-to-phosphorus ratio is one of the most important mineral relationships in horse nutrition.

What Limestone Is

Limestone used in animal feeds is ground calcium carbonate — a highly purified, finely ground form of the same mineral that makes up limestone rock and seashells. It is approximately 38–40% calcium by weight, making it a concentrated calcium source. Feed-grade limestone is free of significant impurities and is highly bioavailable to horses when included in a complete feed.

On labels: limestone, calcium carbonate, ground limestone.

How Limestone Affects Your Horse

Bone and skeletal health: The skeleton of a mature horse contains several kilograms of calcium, deposited primarily as hydroxyapatite — a calcium-phosphate mineral crystal that gives bone its hardness. Adequate calcium intake throughout a horse's life is essential for maintaining bone density and structural integrity. Chronically inadequate calcium intake causes the body to resorb calcium from bone to maintain blood calcium levels — a condition called nutritional secondary hyperparathyroidism, which can lead to bone softening, facial swelling (bighead disease in severe cases), and increased fracture risk.

Growing horses have particularly high calcium requirements because they are simultaneously depositing mineral into a rapidly growing skeleton. Calcium deficiency during growth can cause developmental orthopedic disease — skeletal abnormalities including osteochondrosis (OCD), physitis, and angular limb deformities.

Muscle function: The approximately 1% of body calcium not in bone plays equally critical roles. Calcium ions trigger muscle contraction — when a nerve impulse reaches a muscle fiber, calcium is released inside the cell and binds to the contractile proteins, initiating contraction. After the contraction, calcium is pumped back out. This calcium cycling occurs with every heartbeat and every voluntary muscle movement. Without adequate calcium, muscle contraction is impaired, and in severe deficiency, muscle tremors and weakness can develop.

Nerve transmission: Calcium is involved in the release of neurotransmitters at nerve synapses. Acute, severe calcium deficiency (hypocalcemia) causes increased neuromuscular excitability — muscles and nerves become hypersensitive, leading to muscle tremors, spasms, and in extreme cases, tetany. Hypocalcemia in horses is most commonly seen in mares during lactation ("milk fever") or in endurance horses after strenuous exertion ("thumps" — synchronous diaphragmatic flutter).

The calcium-to-phosphorus ratio: The ratio of calcium to phosphorus in the total diet is one of the most discussed mineral relationships in horse nutrition. The generally recommended ratio is 1.5–2:1 (calcium:phosphorus). Both extremes — too low calcium relative to phosphorus (inverted ratio) or too high calcium relative to phosphorus — can cause problems.

An inverted ratio (more phosphorus than calcium) is the more dangerous condition. Excess dietary phosphorus competitively inhibits calcium absorption in the intestine, effectively reducing the amount of calcium the horse can absorb from its food regardless of how much calcium is present. This is the mechanism behind the calcium deficiency seen in horses fed large amounts of rice bran (very high phosphorus), wheat bran (very high phosphorus), or grain-heavy diets without calcium supplementation.

Very high calcium relative to phosphorus (ratios above 4–5:1) can also impair phosphorus absorption over time, though horses are more tolerant of calcium excess than phosphorus excess. Very high calcium also interferes with magnesium and zinc absorption when extreme.

Limestone in a commercial feed is used to correct the calcium-phosphorus ratio after the grain and protein ingredients have been added. Most grains are low in calcium and moderate in phosphorus — limestone brings the ratio back to an appropriate range.

Reading Limestone on a Feed Label

Limestone itself does not appear on the guaranteed analysis — instead, calcium is listed as a minimum and maximum percentage. The presence of limestone in the ingredient list tells you it is a primary calcium source. When evaluating whether a feed has an appropriate calcium level, check the guaranteed analysis for calcium % (min) and phosphorus % (min) and calculate the ratio: calcium ÷ phosphorus = Ca:P ratio. A ratio between 1.5:1 and 2.5:1 is generally considered appropriate for most horses.

Remember that the feed's Ca:P ratio reflects only the concentrated feed — the total dietary Ca:P ratio depends on the hay and any other forage the horse is eating. Grass hays typically have Ca:P ratios near 1:1 to 1.5:1; alfalfa hay has ratios of 5:1 or higher. The combined ratio of all feeds together determines whether the horse's total diet is appropriately balanced.
