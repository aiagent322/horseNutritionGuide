Soybean Hulls

The outer shell of the soybean — a highly digestible, low-sugar fiber source that is a cornerstone of low-starch horse feed formulation.

Plain-English Summary

Soybean hulls are the outer seed coats removed from soybeans before oil and meal extraction. They are very high in digestible fiber, very low in starch and sugar, and provide energy through hindgut fermentation rather than glucose absorption. This makes them one of the safest energy and fiber sources available for horses with insulin resistance, laminitis, EMS, or Cushing's disease. They are widely used in senior feeds, low-starch feeds, and complete feeds as a hay-replacing fiber. Soybean hulls are not a significant protein, fat, or vitamin source — their value is almost entirely as a highly digestible, metabolically safe fiber.

What Soybean Hulls Are

Soybean hulls are the seed coats (outer shells) of soybeans (Glycine max), removed during the processing of soybeans into soybean oil and soybean meal. This mechanical separation produces a fibrous byproduct that is dried and sold as an animal feed ingredient. Because the hull surrounds the seed rather than being part of the seed's nutritive interior, it contains very little of the starch, protein, or fat found in the soybean itself.

On a feed label, soybean hulls may appear as: soybean hulls, soybean mill feed, soy hulls, or dried soybean hulls.

How Soybean Hulls Affect Your Horse

Hindgut fermentation and safe energy: Like beet pulp, soybean hulls provide energy through fermentable fiber. The fiber is fermented by hindgut microbes into volatile fatty acids (VFAs), which are absorbed and used as energy without causing the blood glucose and insulin spikes associated with starch digestion. This makes soybean hulls an excellent energy source for insulin-resistant horses, laminitis-risk horses, and horses that need calories without grain.

Digestibility: Soybean hulls are notably more digestible than most structural plant fibers. The cell walls are thin and contain relatively little lignin (the indigestible woody component that limits fiber digestibility). The result is that horses can extract significantly more energy from soybean hulls per pound than from mature grass hay or straw. This high digestibility also makes soybean hulls a good fiber source for senior horses with reduced digestive efficiency.

Effect on hindgut health: As a fermentable fiber source, soybean hulls support the microbial community of the cecum and large colon. A consistent supply of fermentable fiber helps maintain hindgut pH stability and microbial diversity, which is associated with better overall digestive health and reduced risk of hindgut dysbiosis.

Low NSC: Soybean hulls typically contain less than 12% NSC on a dry matter basis, with very low sugar and starch fractions. This is substantially lower than most grains and even lower than many grass hays. For horses that require a low-NSC diet, soybean hulls as a primary energy source represent one of the safest choices available.

Satiety and gut fill: Soybean hulls, like other high-fiber feeds, provide gut fill — physical bulk in the digestive tract that contributes to a feeling of satiety. This is particularly relevant for horses on restricted diets that may otherwise develop stable vices related to boredom and hunger.

Nutritional Profile

Soybean hulls are approximately 10–12% crude protein, 2–3% crude fat, and 38–42% crude fiber on a dry matter basis. The crude fiber value is high, but the key characteristic is that this fiber is highly fermentable — unlike the crude fiber in mature hay, which contains more indigestible lignin. The digestible energy content of soybean hulls is approximately 1.0–1.1 Mcal/lb on a dry matter basis — comparable to good-quality grass hay.

Soybean hulls are a modest source of calcium (approximately 0.4–0.5%) and low in phosphorus, giving them a favorable calcium-to-phosphorus ratio. They are not a significant source of most vitamins or trace minerals.

Cautions and Considerations

Not a complete feed component alone: Soybean hulls provide fiber and energy but are not nutritionally complete. Feeds that use soybean hulls as a primary ingredient must include a comprehensive vitamin and mineral package.

Introduce gradually: As with any fiber source, the hindgut microbial population needs 7–14 days to adapt to a new fiber source. Abrupt introduction of large amounts of soybean hulls can cause temporary loose manure as the microbial community adjusts.

Not a hay replacement alone: Soybean hulls provide fermentable fiber but lack the long-stem fiber (effective fiber) that stimulates chewing and saliva production. Horses need long-stem fiber for dental health, gastric buffering, and normal digestive motility. Soybean hulls work best as a fiber supplement alongside hay or in complete feeds that account for total fiber needs.
