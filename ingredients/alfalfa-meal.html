Alfalfa Meal / Dehydrated Alfalfa

A high-protein, high-calcium forage ingredient that adds digestible energy and supports protein status — particularly useful for senior horses, hard keepers, and horses needing gastric buffering.

Plain-English Summary

Alfalfa meal is dried and ground alfalfa (Medicago sativa), one of the most nutrient-dense forage crops available. It provides significantly more protein and calcium than grass hay and is a common ingredient in commercial horse feeds to boost protein content, add calcium, and provide highly palatable, digestible energy. Alfalfa's high protein and calcium content make it valuable for senior horses, young growing horses, and lactating mares — but these same characteristics require attention in horses with certain health conditions. The high calcium content of alfalfa can disrupt calcium-to-phosphorus balance when fed in large amounts alongside other high-calcium ingredients. Alfalfa also has a documented gastric buffering effect that may benefit horses prone to gastric ulcers.

What Alfalfa Meal Is

Alfalfa meal is produced by harvesting alfalfa at peak nutritional value, dehydrating it rapidly to preserve nutrients, and grinding it to a meal or pellet form. The dehydration process — typically using forced-air dryers at controlled temperatures — preserves more carotene (a Vitamin A precursor), protein, and chlorophyll than field-dried hay. Dehydrated alfalfa meal is therefore more nutritionally consistent than field-cured hay, whose quality varies significantly with weather conditions during curing.

On feed labels: alfalfa meal, dehydrated alfalfa meal, sun-cured alfalfa meal, or alfalfa.

How Alfalfa Meal Affects Your Horse

Protein: Alfalfa is one of the highest-protein forage crops, typically containing 17–22% crude protein on a dry matter basis. More importantly, alfalfa protein is of relatively good quality — it contains meaningful amounts of lysine, the first-limiting amino acid for horses, at higher concentrations than most grass hays. When alfalfa meal appears early in a feed's ingredient list, it contributes substantially to the feed's crude protein value.

For horses with elevated protein needs — growing horses, lactating mares, senior horses with muscle wasting, and horses recovering from illness or injury — alfalfa meal is a useful protein source. For mature horses at light work with normal body condition, the high protein content of alfalfa-heavy feeds means more nitrogen must be excreted as urea, increasing water consumption and urine production. This is not harmful for healthy horses with kidney function, but it is worth noting.

Calcium: Alfalfa is exceptionally high in calcium, typically 1.2–1.8% on a dry matter basis compared to 0.3–0.5% for grass hay. This calcium richness can benefit horses with high calcium needs (young growing horses, lactating mares) but requires attention in other contexts. The ideal dietary calcium-to-phosphorus ratio for horses is approximately 1.5–2:1. Grain-heavy diets are already low in calcium relative to phosphorus; adding alfalfa can correct this imbalance. However, when alfalfa-based feeds are fed alongside alfalfa hay, total dietary calcium can become very high. Chronically excessive calcium intake may interfere with the absorption of other minerals, particularly phosphorus, magnesium, and zinc.

Gastric buffering: Research has shown that alfalfa hay and alfalfa-based feeds have a gastric buffering effect — the high calcium and protein content of alfalfa helps neutralize gastric acid. Studies have found that horses fed alfalfa hay had lower gastric ulcer scores than horses fed grass hay alone. For horses prone to squamous gastric ulcers (the most common form, affecting the upper portion of the stomach), including alfalfa in the diet — whether as hay, a component of a commercial feed, or both — may provide protective benefit. This effect is particularly relevant for performance horses under training stress, horses with a history of ulcers, and horses that go extended periods without forage.

Energy: Alfalfa is more energy-dense than most grass hays, providing approximately 1.0–1.2 Mcal of digestible energy per pound on a dry matter basis. This energy comes primarily from digestible fiber (pectin and hemicellulose) rather than starch, making alfalfa a relatively safe energy source for metabolic horses compared to grain — though its NSC content (typically 10–18% depending on cutting and conditions) is higher than beet pulp or soybean hulls and should be verified for horses with tight NSC restrictions.

Palatability: Alfalfa is highly palatable — most horses find it very appealing. This makes alfalfa-containing feeds useful for horses that are picky eaters, horses recovering from illness with reduced appetite, and young horses being introduced to concentrates.

Cautions and Considerations

High calcium with alfalfa hay: Feeding an alfalfa-meal-based commercial feed alongside alfalfa hay means the horse is receiving two high-calcium sources. Review total diet calcium-to-phosphorus balance if alfalfa is a major component of both the feed and the hay ration.

NSC in metabolic horses: Alfalfa's NSC is substantially lower than grain but higher than beet pulp and soybean hulls. For horses with very tight NSC restrictions (below 10%), verify the tested NSC of any alfalfa-containing feed with the manufacturer.

Horses with kidney disease: The high protein content of alfalfa-based feeds means more nitrogen load on the kidneys. Horses with chronic kidney disease or reduced kidney function should be evaluated by a veterinarian before being fed high-alfalfa diets.

"Hot" behavior: Some horse owners report that alfalfa-fed horses are more excitable or "hot." While this perception is common, the scientific evidence for a direct behavioral effect of alfalfa is limited. The higher energy density of alfalfa could contribute to excess energy if horses are overfed relative to their workload, which might manifest behaviorally. Managing total caloric intake is the appropriate response.
