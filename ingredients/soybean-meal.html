Soybean Meal

The most widely used concentrated protein source in commercial horse feed — valued for its high lysine content and consistent amino acid profile.

Plain-English Summary

Soybean meal is the high-protein residue left after soybean oil is extracted from whole soybeans. It contains approximately 44–48% crude protein and is particularly valued in horse nutrition for its lysine content — lysine is the first-limiting amino acid for horses, meaning it is most often the amino acid in shortest supply relative to the horse's needs. When a commercial feed lists soybean meal as a primary ingredient, it is the main reason that feed has a meaningful crude protein value. Soybean meal is well-digested by horses, though it is not a forage substitute and contributes little to fiber or energy beyond its protein value.

What Soybean Meal Is

Soybean meal is produced by mechanically pressing or chemically extracting oil from whole soybeans, then drying and grinding the remaining high-protein cake. Most commercial soybean meal is solvent-extracted using hexane, producing a product with very little residual fat (approximately 1–3%). Expeller-pressed soybean meal retains more fat (approximately 5–7%) and is sometimes preferred in equine feeds for the slightly higher fat content, though both forms provide similar protein quality.

Heat treatment during processing is critical — raw soybeans contain trypsin inhibitors and other anti-nutritional factors that impair protein digestion. Proper heat treatment during processing deactivates these factors, making soybean meal protein highly available to the horse.

On labels: soybean meal, solvent extracted soybean meal, expeller pressed soybean meal.

How Soybean Meal Affects Your Horse

Protein and amino acids: The horse's body requires protein not as a whole molecule but as individual amino acids — the building blocks that proteins are assembled from. Of the approximately 20 amino acids horses use, ten are considered essential, meaning the horse cannot synthesize them in adequate amounts and must obtain them from the diet. Lysine is the most important of these for horses because it is the amino acid most commonly deficient in typical hay-based diets.

Soybean meal contains approximately 2.8–3.0% lysine on a dry matter basis — substantially higher than most other protein sources used in horse feeds. This high lysine content is why soybean meal is so widely used: it efficiently addresses the most common amino acid limitation in equine diets. When lysine is limiting, the horse cannot synthesize protein at the rate needed for muscle development, repair, coat quality, or immune function, regardless of total crude protein intake.

Muscle development and maintenance: Adequate lysine from soybean meal supports muscle protein synthesis — the process by which the horse builds and repairs skeletal muscle. This is relevant for young growing horses building muscle mass, performance horses experiencing muscle breakdown and repair from training, senior horses fighting age-related muscle loss (sarcopenia), and horses recovering from illness or injury with muscle wasting.

The distinction between crude protein percentage and amino acid quality is important here. A feed can have a high crude protein percentage from a poor-quality protein source (one low in lysine) and still fail to support adequate muscle development. Soybean meal's lysine content makes it a high-quality protein source, not just a high-quantity one.

Methionine and threonine: Soybean meal also contains methionine (approximately 0.6%) and threonine (approximately 1.7%), the second- and third-limiting amino acids for horses. When a commercial feed's guaranteed analysis lists lysine, methionine, and threonine separately, it indicates the manufacturer is verifying the quality of the protein they are providing — soybean meal is usually the source behind those guarantees.

Digestibility: Properly heat-treated soybean meal is highly digestible by horses — protein digestibility is typically in the range of 80–90%. This compares favorably to other protein sources. However, horses digest protein primarily in the small intestine, and when protein intake significantly exceeds requirements, excess amino acids are deaminated (stripped of their nitrogen group) and the nitrogen is excreted as urea in urine. High-protein feeds lead to increased water consumption and increased urinary nitrogen excretion, which can increase stall ammonia levels.

Cautions and Considerations

Protein requirements vary by horse: Adult horses at maintenance need relatively little crude protein — approximately 8–10% of total diet dry matter. Growing horses, lactating mares, and horses in heavy work need more. Overfeeding protein is not directly harmful to healthy horses but increases metabolic nitrogen load. For horses with kidney disease, high-protein feeds including soybean meal should be used cautiously and under veterinary guidance.

Soy sensitivity: True soy allergy or sensitivity in horses is considered uncommon but has been reported. Signs include hives, skin reactions, or digestive upset. If a horse shows consistent adverse reactions to feeds containing soybean meal, alternative protein sources such as canola meal or linseed meal should be considered.

GMO status: The majority of soybeans grown in North America are genetically modified. For horse owners who prefer non-GMO feeds, this may be a consideration — some feed manufacturers offer non-GMO certified options.
