Grain / High-Starch Energy — Metabolic Caution

Corn (Maize)

The most energy-dense and highest-starch grain used in horse feeds — effective for horses with high caloric needs, inappropriate for horses with metabolic conditions.

Plain-English Summary

Corn (maize) is the highest-starch, most calorie-dense grain commonly used in horse feeds. It provides approximately 70% starch on a dry matter basis and roughly 1.7 Mcal of digestible energy per pound — substantially more energy per pound than oats or barley. For horses with very high energy demands (racehorses, horses in sustained heavy work, hard keepers that cannot maintain weight), corn can effectively provide the calories they need. For horses with insulin resistance, EMS, laminitis, Cushing's disease, PSSM, or any condition requiring low NSC, corn is contraindicated. Its high starch content causes significant blood glucose and insulin spikes and presents real risk of hindgut acidosis if meal sizes are large.

What Corn Is

Corn (Zea mays) is a cereal grain originating in the Americas and now one of the most widely grown crops worldwide. The corn kernel contains a large starchy endosperm surrounded by a thin outer pericarp, with a small germ containing fat and protein. Unlike oats, corn has no significant fibrous hull — the pericarp is thin and does not contribute meaningful protective fiber to moderate starch digestion rate.

Corn in horse feeds may appear as: ground corn, cracked corn, flaked corn, steam-flaked corn, corn, or maize. Steam-flaking is the processing method that most dramatically improves starch digestibility.

How Corn Affects Your Horse

Energy density: Corn provides more digestible energy per pound than any other common horse grain. This caloric density is corn's primary value in horse feeds — it is used when high energy output is needed in a relatively small feed volume. This matters for horses in hard training that cannot consume enough hay and lower-calorie feed to meet their energy needs, and for hard-keeping horses that lose weight on oat-based feeds.

Starch and insulin response: Corn's starch is primarily composed of amylopectin — a highly branched starch structure that is digested more rapidly than the amylose starch more prevalent in oats. The result is that corn produces a faster and higher blood glucose and insulin peak than oats fed in equivalent amounts. For healthy horses in work that need rapid glycogen replenishment, this is not necessarily a problem. For metabolic horses, this rapid insulin spike is the primary mechanism of harm.

Hindgut overflow risk: Corn's high starch content in a kernel with no protective hull means that large corn meals are more likely to overwhelm small intestinal starch-digesting capacity than equivalent oat meals. Starch reaching the hindgut ferments rapidly, producing lactic acid and reducing pH. Hindgut acidosis from grain overload can cause colic, laminitis, and behavioral changes. Managing corn meal size is critical — never feed more than the horse's small intestine can handle in a single sitting, and the safe upper limit varies by individual horse and processing form.

Processing and digestibility: Whole corn kernels are poorly digested by horses — the pericarp resists mechanical breakdown and the starch remains largely unavailable. Cracking or grinding increases starch surface area and improves digestibility substantially. Steam-flaking — treating corn with steam and then rolling it flat — gelatinizes the starch, making it highly available and significantly improving digestibility and energy extraction. Steam-flaked corn is the most digestible form and is most commonly used in premium horse feeds and in feeds designed for performance horses.

Behavior and excitability: Horses on high-corn diets are sometimes described as "hot" or excitable. Whether this is a direct physiological effect of corn's glycemic response or a function of excess total caloric intake is debated, but the practical observation is consistent enough that high-corn feeds are generally avoided for horses prone to excitability, barn horses in limited turnout, or horses in irregular work where caloric expenditure varies widely.

Cautions

Not appropriate for metabolic horses (IR, EMS, laminitis, Cushing's, PSSM). Feed in multiple small meals — never a large single grain meal. Match caloric intake to caloric expenditure. Introduce gradually. Be aware of NSC contribution when evaluating total diet for any horse.
