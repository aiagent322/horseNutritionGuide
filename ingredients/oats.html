Grain / Starch Energy — Metabolic Caution

Oats

The most traditional grain in horse feeding — safer than corn or barley in terms of starch digestibility, but still a significant starch source that is not appropriate for all horses.

Plain-English Summary

Oats have been fed to horses for centuries and remain one of the most commonly used grains worldwide. Compared to corn and barley, oats have a lower starch content and a more fibrous hull that slows digestion, making them somewhat safer from a starch overload perspective. However, oats are still a meaningful starch source — they are not appropriate for horses with insulin resistance, EMS, laminitis history, Cushing's disease, or PSSM without veterinary guidance. Oats provide quick-burning energy that is appropriate for horses in active work, but that same readily available starch is a liability for metabolic horses. The traditional reputation of oats as a "safe" grain should not be interpreted to mean they are safe for all horses in all situations.

What Oats Are

Oats (Avena sativa) are a cereal grain with a distinctive fibrous hull that surrounds the oat groat (the inner seed). This hull is a distinguishing characteristic compared to other common horse grains — corn and barley kernels have minimal protective covering, while oats retain a fibrous coat that represents approximately 25–30% of the grain's weight. The hull contributes to oat's higher crude fiber content and its slower rate of starch digestion compared to corn.

Oats used in horse feeds may be: whole (unhulled), crimped (rolled to break the hull), ground, or steam-flaked. Processing affects digestibility — crimped or steam-flaked oats have improved starch digestibility compared to whole oats because the processing breaks the hull and makes the starch more accessible to digestive enzymes. On labels: oats, whole oats, rolled oats, crimped oats, ground oats, steam-flaked oats.

How Oats Affect Your Horse

Starch and energy: Oats contain approximately 40–50% starch on a dry matter basis — lower than corn (approximately 70%) but still a substantial starch load. The starch in oats is digested primarily in the small intestine by amylase enzymes, producing glucose that is absorbed into the bloodstream. This causes a rise in blood glucose and a corresponding insulin response. For metabolically healthy horses in work, this response is normal and appropriate — the glucose provides rapidly available energy for muscle contraction.

The fibrous hull of oats moderates the rate of starch digestion somewhat compared to corn, but this moderating effect is limited. Processing (crimping, rolling, steam-flaking) improves starch availability and digestibility, which means processed oats actually have a more rapid effect on blood glucose than whole oats, not less. This is why feeding recommendations for grain-sensitive horses should specify not just the grain type but also the processing form.

Small intestinal capacity and hindgut overflow: A key concern with starchy grains is exceeding the small intestine's capacity to digest starch, allowing undigested starch to reach the hindgut. In the hindgut, starch is rapidly fermented by bacteria, producing lactic acid and reducing pH — a condition called hindgut acidosis. This disrupts the microbial community, can damage the gut lining, and is associated with increased risk of colic and laminitis. The risk of hindgut starch overflow increases with meal size — larger grain meals are more likely to overwhelm small intestinal capacity. For this reason, grain should be fed in multiple small meals rather than one or two large ones.

Metabolic horses: Despite their lower starch content compared to corn, oats are not safe for horses with insulin resistance, EMS, laminitis history, Cushing's disease, or PSSM. The insulin response to oat starch is real and significant enough to worsen insulin dysregulation in sensitive horses. For these horses, fiber-based energy sources (beet pulp, soybean hulls) and fat sources are the appropriate alternatives.

Performance horses: For horses in sustained, high-intensity work — racehorses, eventers, endurance horses, reining horses — the rapidly available glucose from oat starch can fuel muscle glycogen replenishment. In this context, oats serve their traditional purpose effectively. The key is matching starch intake to energy expenditure and managing meal size to avoid digestive upset.

Oat hull fiber: The hull contributes approximately 10–12% crude fiber to whole oats. This fiber provides some gut fill and supports intestinal motility, though it is not as fermentable as the fiber in beet pulp or soybean hulls. Oat hulls, when separated, are sometimes used as a lower-calorie fiber source in feeds (listed as "oat hulls" or "oat fiber" on labels).

Processing Forms and Their Effects

Whole oats have the lowest starch digestibility — some starch passes through without being fully digested, particularly in older horses or horses with reduced salivation. Horses need to chew whole oats thoroughly for maximum benefit.

Crimped/rolled oats have improved digestibility compared to whole oats because the hull is broken and the starch is more accessible. These are the most common form in commercial textured feeds.

Steam-flaked oats have the highest starch digestibility — steam cooking gelatinizes the starch, making it much more accessible to amylase. This improves energy extraction but also increases the glycemic response.

Ground oats are finely milled and highly digestible but may be consumed too quickly, reducing saliva production and chewing time.

Cautions

Not appropriate for: horses with IR, EMS, laminitis history, PPID/Cushing's, or PSSM without veterinary guidance. Feed in multiple small meals. Limit single-meal grain intake. Introduce gradually when changing grain feeding programs.
