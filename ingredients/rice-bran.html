Fat / Energy Source

Rice Bran

A high-fat byproduct of rice milling that provides concentrated energy without starch — popular for hard keepers and performance horses, but with an important mineral balance consideration.

Plain-English Summary

Rice bran is the outer layer of the rice grain removed during milling to produce white rice. It is high in fat — typically 15–22% — making it one of the most calorie-dense ingredients used in horse feeds. Because that energy comes from fat rather than starch, rice bran does not cause the blood glucose and insulin spikes associated with grain feeding, making it a useful calorie source for horses that need extra energy without added starch. The critical issue with rice bran is its calcium-to-phosphorus ratio: it is extremely high in phosphorus relative to calcium, which can invert the ideal dietary ratio and interfere with calcium absorption when fed in large amounts. Most commercial rice bran products are balanced with added calcium to correct this. Always verify that any rice bran product or rice bran-containing feed addresses this balance.

What Rice Bran Is

Rice bran is the outer bran layer and germ of the rice kernel, separated during the milling process that converts brown rice to white rice. It contains the majority of the rice grain's fat, fiber, vitamins, and minerals. Raw rice bran spoils rapidly — within hours to days of milling — because it contains an enzyme called lipase that rapidly oxidizes the fat, causing rancidity. Commercial rice bran products for horses are therefore stabilized through heat treatment (extrusion or other processes) that deactivates the lipase enzyme and extends shelf life. Always use stabilized rice bran; unstabilized rice bran is nutritionally inferior and potentially harmful.

On labels: rice bran, stabilized rice bran, heat-stabilized rice bran.

How Rice Bran Affects Your Horse

Fat energy without starch: Fat provides approximately 2.25 times more energy per gram than carbohydrates. Rice bran's high fat content (15–22%) makes it one of the most calorie-dense ingredients available for horses. When a feed includes significant rice bran, it is primarily there to add calories — and to add them without the starch load that grain brings. For hard keepers, performance horses needing sustained energy, and horses requiring weight gain without grain sensitivity risks, rice bran is a highly effective energy source.

Fat digestion and absorption occurs in the small intestine via a different pathway than starch digestion. Fat does not stimulate a significant insulin response, making rice bran-based energy appropriate even for horses with insulin resistance, provided the total diet NSC is managed appropriately.

Omega-6 fatty acids and coat: Rice bran is rich in omega-6 fatty acids, particularly linoleic acid, which supports skin barrier function and coat quality. Horses fed rice bran often show improved coat shine and skin condition, particularly if they were previously on a low-fat diet. Rice bran does not provide significant omega-3 fatty acids — flaxseed or fish oil are better omega-3 sources.

The phosphorus problem: Rice bran contains approximately 1.5–2.0% phosphorus but only 0.05–0.1% calcium — an extremely inverted calcium-to-phosphorus ratio of approximately 0.05:1, compared to the ideal dietary ratio of 1.5–2:1. When fed in significant amounts without calcium correction, rice bran can create a serious dietary phosphorus excess that interferes with calcium absorption and bone mineralization. Chronic calcium deficiency from inverted Ca:P ratio has been associated with nutritional secondary hyperparathyroidism — the body draws calcium from bones to maintain blood calcium levels.

Commercial rice bran products intended for horses typically have calcium added to correct this imbalance — look for a balanced Ca:P ratio on the guaranteed analysis. Rice bran used as an ingredient in a commercial feed should have its calcium contribution accounted for in the feed's overall mineral balance. When evaluating any rice bran product or rice bran-containing feed, check the calcium and phosphorus values on the guaranteed analysis and verify the ratio is appropriate.

Phytic acid and mineral absorption: Rice bran contains phytic acid (phytate), a compound that can bind minerals including zinc, iron, and calcium, reducing their bioavailability. This is an additional reason to ensure adequate mineral intake when feeding rice bran in significant quantities.

Cautions and Considerations

Always use stabilized rice bran — unstabilized product becomes rancid rapidly and can cause digestive upset and reduced palatability.

Calcium balance — verify the Ca:P ratio of any rice bran product or rice bran-containing feed before use, especially if feeding large amounts.

Gradual introduction — introduce fat gradually over 2–3 weeks to allow the horse's digestive system to adapt to higher fat levels. Horses on low-fat diets may develop loose manure initially.

Storage — even stabilized rice bran can become rancid over time. Store in a cool, dry location and use before the best-by date. Rancid fat has reduced nutritional value and may reduce feed palatability.
