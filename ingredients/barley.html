Grain / Starch Energy — Metabolic Caution

Barley

A high-energy grain with starch content between oats and corn — widely used in European horse feeds, less common in North American formulations.

Plain-English Summary

Barley (Hordeum vulgare) is a cereal grain with approximately 55–65% starch content — higher than oats (40–50%) but lower than corn (approximately 70%). It provides meaningful caloric density and is widely used in European horse nutrition, though less commonly seen in North American commercial horse feeds. Like all grains, barley's starch causes blood glucose and insulin increases upon digestion and is not appropriate for horses with metabolic conditions. Barley must be processed before feeding — whole barley is very poorly digested by horses, and the hard kernel resists breakdown in the digestive tract. Rolling, crimping, or steam-flaking improves digestibility substantially.

What Barley Is

Barley is a cereal grain that may be two-rowed or six-rowed (referring to the arrangement of kernels on the plant head). Feed barley is typically six-rowed, higher in protein than two-rowed malting barley. Like oats, barley can be hulled (with the fibrous hull attached) or hull-less, though most barley in horse feeds is hulled. The hull contributes some fiber but is less fibrous than the oat hull. Barley protein content is approximately 11–15% on a dry matter basis — similar to oats and slightly higher than corn.

On labels: barley, rolled barley, steam-rolled barley, steam-flaked barley, whole barley.

How Barley Affects Your Horse

Energy and starch: Barley provides more digestible energy per pound than oats but less than corn. Its starch is primarily amylopectin (like corn) with a smaller amylose fraction. The glycemic response to barley — the rise in blood glucose and insulin following consumption — is meaningful and intermediate between oats and corn. For metabolic horses, barley presents the same concerns as other high-starch grains.

Processing requirement: Whole barley is notably poorly digested by horses. The hard, vitreous endosperm resists enzymatic breakdown, and a significant proportion of whole barley starch can pass through the small intestine undigested, reaching the hindgut and contributing to acidosis. Rolling, crimping, or steam-flaking is not optional — it is a nutritional necessity for barley to be safely and efficiently used as an energy source. Steam-flaking achieves the best digestibility by gelatinizing the starch.

Beta-glucan fiber: Barley contains beta-glucan, a soluble fiber that slows starch digestion by increasing digesta viscosity. This moderates the glycemic response to barley somewhat compared to equivalent corn feeding. However, this effect is modest and does not make barley appropriate for metabolic horses.

Hindgut health: As with any high-starch grain, the primary digestive risk is hindgut overflow and acidosis from inadequate small intestinal starch digestion. Proper processing and appropriate meal sizes reduce this risk but do not eliminate it.

Performance horses: Barley is a traditional performance horse grain in European equestrian traditions. It provides high energy density with slightly lower starch than corn and reasonable palatability. In the context of a balanced diet with appropriate total grain intake divided into multiple daily meals, barley performs well for horses in sustained heavy work.

Cautions

Never feed whole barley to horses without processing. Not appropriate for metabolic horses. Feed in multiple small meals. Introduce gradually over 7–14 days when adding to the diet.
