Flaxseed / Linseed

The richest plant source of omega-3 fatty acids for horses — supporting coat quality, reducing inflammation, and improving the fatty acid balance of the equine diet.

Plain-English Summary

Flaxseed (also called linseed) is the seed of the flax plant (Linum usitatissimum). It is the richest plant source of alpha-linolenic acid (ALA), the primary omega-3 fatty acid found in plant foods. Horses evolved eating fresh grass, which is naturally high in omega-3s — modern hay and grain diets are relatively poor in omega-3s and higher in omega-6s. Flaxseed helps restore a more favorable omega-3 to omega-6 ratio in the horse's diet, which is associated with reduced inflammatory signaling, improved coat quality, and better skin condition. For maximum benefit, flaxseed must be ground or heat-processed before feeding — whole flaxseeds pass through the digestive tract largely intact, with much of their oil content unabsorbed.

What Flaxseed Is

Flaxseed is a small, brown or golden oilseed containing approximately 40–45% fat, 20–25% protein, 25–30% carbohydrate (primarily fiber), and significant amounts of lignans (plant compounds with antioxidant properties). The fat content is predominantly alpha-linolenic acid (ALA), an omega-3 fatty acid, comprising approximately 55–60% of the total fat. This makes flaxseed the most omega-3-rich plant ingredient commonly used in horse nutrition.

On labels: flaxseed, linseed, ground flaxseed, stabilized ground flaxseed, flaxseed meal. Stabilized ground flaxseed has been processed to deactivate the enzyme lipase, which otherwise causes rapid rancidity of the ground product.

How Flaxseed Affects Your Horse

The omega-3 to omega-6 ratio: Omega-3 and omega-6 fatty acids are both essential — the horse's body cannot synthesize them and must obtain them from the diet. However, they have opposing effects on inflammatory pathways: omega-6 fatty acids tend to promote inflammatory signaling, while omega-3 fatty acids tend to resolve and reduce it. Both types of inflammation are necessary and appropriate in the right context, but a diet chronically high in omega-6s relative to omega-3s is associated with increased inflammatory tone throughout the body.

Fresh pasture grass contains a relatively balanced omega-3 to omega-6 ratio. Hay loses much of its omega-3 content during drying and storage. Grains (corn, oats, barley) and grain byproducts are predominantly omega-6 sources. As a result, horses on hay and grain diets typically have a much higher omega-6 to omega-3 ratio than horses at pasture. Flaxseed's high ALA content helps rebalance this ratio.

Anti-inflammatory effects: ALA from flaxseed can be converted by the horse's body into longer-chain omega-3 fatty acids including EPA (eicosapentaenoic acid) and DHA (docosahexaenoic acid), which are the active mediators of omega-3's anti-inflammatory effects. However, horses convert ALA to EPA and DHA relatively inefficiently — the conversion rate in horses is not precisely established but is known to be limited, as in other mammals. Despite this limitation, flaxseed supplementation has been associated with measurable improvements in inflammatory markers and immune function in some equine studies.

For horses with chronic inflammatory conditions — recurrent airway obstruction (heaves/equine asthma), skin conditions, joint inflammation, or horses under the inflammatory stress of heavy training — the omega-3 contribution of flaxseed may offer meaningful benefit. Fish oil (which contains pre-formed EPA and DHA) bypasses the conversion step and may be more effective for specific inflammatory conditions, but flaxseed remains a practical and palatable plant-based omega-3 source.

Coat and skin quality: Omega-3 fatty acids are integral to the structure of skin cell membranes and play a role in maintaining skin barrier function. Horses supplemented with flaxseed or other omega-3 sources frequently show improvement in coat shine, reduced flakiness, and improved skin moisture retention. This effect is often visible within 6–10 weeks of consistent supplementation. It is one of the most commonly observed and reported benefits of flaxseed in horses.

Immune function: Omega-3 fatty acids influence immune cell function and cytokine production. In horses, supplementation with omega-3-rich sources including flaxseed has been associated with altered immune responses in some studies, including modified response to vaccination antigens. The clinical significance of these immune effects varies by individual horse and condition.

Form matters — whole vs ground: Whole flaxseeds have a hard seed coat that is largely impermeable to digestive enzymes. Studies in horses and other species have shown that whole flaxseeds are poorly digested, with much of the seed passing through the gastrointestinal tract intact. Grinding flaxseed breaks open the seed coat and makes the oil available for digestion and absorption. Heat processing (as in extruded or steam-processed feeds) also improves availability. For flaxseed to provide its omega-3 benefits, it must be fed ground or heat-processed — not whole.

Cautions and Considerations

Prussic acid (cyanogenic glycosides): Raw flaxseed contains small amounts of cyanogenic glycosides — compounds that can release hydrogen cyanide under certain conditions. The amount in properly processed feed-grade flaxseed is not considered a health risk for horses at normal feeding rates. The concern is theoretical at typical supplementation levels and does not apply to heat-processed commercial feed products.

Rancidity: Ground flaxseed goes rancid rapidly due to its high unsaturated fat content. Stabilized ground flaxseed (treated to deactivate lipase) has a longer shelf life but should still be stored in cool, dry conditions. Rancid flaxseed has reduced omega-3 content and may reduce palatability. Whole flaxseed is more stable during storage.

Caloric contribution: Flaxseed is calorie-dense due to its high fat content. For easy keepers, large amounts of flaxseed may contribute unwanted calories — consider the total fat content of the diet.
