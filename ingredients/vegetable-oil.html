Fat / Energy Source

Vegetable Oil

A concentrated, starch-free calorie source that supports weight gain, coat quality, and sustained energy — one of the most calorie-dense ingredients in horse feeds.

Plain-English Summary

Vegetable oil is a concentrated source of fat calories — providing approximately 2.25 times more energy per gram than carbohydrates. When added to horse feeds, it primarily serves to increase caloric density without adding starch or sugar. This makes it particularly valuable for hard keepers, performance horses needing sustained energy, and horses that require extra calories but have metabolic conditions that limit grain intake. Vegetable oil also contributes to coat shine and skin condition. The term "vegetable oil" on a label is nonspecific — it may be soybean oil, canola oil, sunflower oil, or a blend, and the source determines the omega-3 to omega-6 fatty acid profile of the oil. Most vegetable oils used in horse feeds are predominantly omega-6 sources.

What Vegetable Oil Is

Vegetable oil is fat extracted from plant seeds or fruits. In horse feeds, the most commonly used vegetable oils are soybean oil, canola oil, corn oil, and sunflower oil. All provide approximately 120 kcal per tablespoon (approximately 9 grams of fat per tablespoon), making them the most calorie-dense ingredient available per unit volume. All are liquid at room temperature (or near room temperature) and are added to commercial feeds by spraying or mixing during manufacturing.

On labels: vegetable oil, soybean oil, canola oil, corn oil, sunflower oil, blended vegetable oil.

How Vegetable Oil Affects Your Horse

Energy without starch or sugar: Fat digestion in horses occurs in the small intestine through a well-established pathway involving bile acids and lipase enzymes. The energy from fat is delivered via a different biochemical pathway than glucose from starch — it does not cause the blood glucose and insulin spikes associated with grain feeding. This makes vegetable oil one of the safest calorie-dense ingredients for insulin-resistant horses and horses with metabolic conditions, provided the total diet composition is appropriate.

For performance horses, the high caloric density of fat means that needed calories can be provided in a smaller total feed volume. Horses have a finite capacity for feed intake, and in horses with very high energy demands, substituting some grain calories with fat calories can be an effective strategy for meeting energy needs without overloading the digestive system with starch.

Fat adaptation: Horses' digestive and metabolic systems adapt to higher fat intakes over time. Horses beginning on low-fat diets should have fat introduced gradually over 2–3 weeks. During this adaptation period, the digestive system upregulates fat-handling capacity. Adapted horses can efficiently utilize higher fat intakes; unadapted horses may develop loose manure when fat is added too abruptly.

Coat and skin quality: Dietary fat provides essential fatty acids that are incorporated into skin cell membranes and contribute to coat quality. Horses on adequate fat intake typically show improved coat shine, reduced dry flakiness, and better overall skin condition. This effect is commonly observed within 4–8 weeks of adding oil to the diet and is one of the most easily visible benefits of fat supplementation.

Omega-6 fatty acids: Most vegetable oils — soybean, corn, sunflower — are predominantly omega-6 fatty acid sources. While omega-6s are essential, modern horse diets are already relatively high in omega-6s relative to omega-3s. Adding large amounts of omega-6-rich oils can further shift the omega-6 to omega-3 ratio in an unfavorable direction. Canola oil has a more favorable omega-3 content than most other vegetable oils used in horse feeds, though it is still predominantly omega-6. For horses where omega-3 balance is a priority (horses with inflammatory conditions, horses without pasture access), flaxseed oil or fish oil provides a better omega-3 profile than most vegetable oils.

Gastric health: There is some evidence that dietary fat, including added vegetable oil, may have a mildly protective effect on the gastric mucosa. Fat slows gastric emptying, which may reduce the extent to which stomach acid contacts the squamous mucosa of the upper stomach — the region most susceptible to ulcers. This effect is not as well-established as that of alfalfa but may contribute a modest protective benefit.

Cautions and Considerations

Caloric excess: Vegetable oil is very calorie-dense. Easy keepers and overweight horses do not benefit from added fat unless it replaces an equivalent calorie source — adding oil to the diet of an easy keeper without reducing other caloric sources will contribute to weight gain.

Rancidity: Vegetable oils can become rancid with oxidation, particularly when exposed to heat, light, and air. Rancid fat has reduced nutritional value and may reduce palatability. Commercial feeds containing vegetable oil typically include antioxidants (such as mixed tocopherols or vitamin E) to extend shelf life. Use feeds before their best-by date and store in cool, dry conditions.

Source transparency: "Vegetable oil" on a label does not specify the source. If the omega fatty acid profile is important for your horse's management, contact the manufacturer to determine the specific oil(s) used.
