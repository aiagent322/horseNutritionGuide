Fiber / Energy Source

Beet Pulp

A highly digestible fiber that provides safe, sustained energy — one of the most useful ingredients in horse feed formulation.

Plain-English Summary

Beet pulp is what remains after the sugar is extracted from sugar beets during sugar production. Despite the name, it is very low in sugar. It provides energy through fermentable fiber rather than starch, making it one of the safest energy sources available for horses — including those with insulin resistance, laminitis history, or metabolic concerns. It is widely used in senior feeds, low-starch feeds, and complete feeds. It is not a significant source of protein, calcium, or most vitamins, so it works best as part of a balanced diet rather than as a standalone feed.

What Beet Pulp Is

Beet pulp is a byproduct of the sugar beet (Beta vulgaris) processing industry. When sugar beets are processed to extract sucrose, the remaining fibrous material — the pulp — is dried and sold as an animal feed ingredient. Nearly all of the sugar content has been removed during processing, leaving behind a fiber-rich material that is very different in composition from the original sugar beet.

Beet pulp is typically available in two forms: shreds (long fibrous strands) and pellets (compressed and ground). Both are nutritionally similar. Some beet pulp products are dried with molasses added back for palatability — these are often labeled "beet pulp with molasses" and are meaningfully higher in sugar than plain beet pulp. Horse owners managing metabolic conditions should specifically seek plain, unsweetened beet pulp.

On a horse feed label, beet pulp may appear as: beet pulp, dried beet pulp, sugar beet pulp, or beet pulp shreds.

How Beet Pulp Affects Your Horse

The primary effect of beet pulp in the horse's body is as a source of fermentable fiber. Unlike starch and sugar — which are digested by enzymes in the small intestine and absorbed as glucose — the fiber in beet pulp passes to the hindgut (cecum and large colon), where it is fermented by the microbial population that lives there. This fermentation produces volatile fatty acids (VFAs), which the horse absorbs and uses as an energy source.

This distinction matters enormously for horse health. When large amounts of starch reach the hindgut undigested (as can happen with high-grain diets), they ferment rapidly, producing lactic acid and reducing pH — a condition known as hindgut acidosis. Beet pulp fiber ferments much more slowly and consistently, without producing the acid spike that starch does. This makes beet pulp a genuinely gut-friendly energy source.

On blood sugar and insulin: Because beet pulp energy is delivered via VFA absorption rather than glucose absorption, it does not cause the same insulin response that starch and sugar do. This is why beet pulp is widely recommended for horses with insulin resistance (IR), equine metabolic syndrome (EMS), laminitis history, and PPID/Cushing's disease. The glycemic impact of beet pulp is substantially lower than that of grain-based feeds.

On digestive health: The fermentable fiber in beet pulp supports a healthy and diverse hindgut microbial population. Horses fed diets that include beet pulp tend to maintain better hindgut pH stability. For horses prone to hindgut upset, loose manure, or who have recently undergone antibiotic treatment, beet pulp can be a useful dietary component.

On body weight and condition: Beet pulp is moderately energy-dense for a fiber source — roughly comparable to good-quality grass hay on an energy basis. It is commonly used to add calories to the diet of hard keepers and senior horses without the risks associated with high-starch grain feeding. However, it is not as calorie-dense as fat supplements or high-grain feeds, so horses with very high energy needs (heavy work, lactation) may need additional energy sources alongside beet pulp.

On hydration: Beet pulp, particularly when fed soaked, can contribute meaningfully to daily water intake. This is beneficial for horses prone to impaction colic, those in hot climates, or horses that are reluctant drinkers. Soaking beet pulp also slows consumption, which is beneficial for horses that eat rapidly.

Nutritional Profile

Plain dried beet pulp is approximately 8–10% crude protein, 0.5–1.0% crude fat, and 18–22% crude fiber on a dry matter basis. It is notably high in calcium (approximately 0.6–0.8%) relative to phosphorus (approximately 0.1%), giving it an inverted calcium-to-phosphorus ratio. This is worth noting for horses receiving large amounts — when beet pulp makes up a major portion of the diet, calcium and phosphorus balance across the total diet should be reviewed.

Beet pulp is not a meaningful source of most vitamins or trace minerals. Feeds that use beet pulp as a primary ingredient need to be supplemented with a vitamin and mineral package to be nutritionally complete.

The digestible energy content of plain beet pulp is approximately 1.1–1.2 Mcal/lb on a dry matter basis — roughly comparable to good-quality grass hay and lower than grains like corn or oats.

Reading Beet Pulp on a Feed Label

On commercial horse feed labels, beet pulp listed among the first three to five ingredients indicates it is a primary energy and fiber source in the formula. When it appears later in the list, it is present in smaller amounts, possibly as a minor fiber source or as a processing aid.

Look carefully for the distinction between plain beet pulp and beet pulp with molasses. The former is very low in sugar; the latter has molasses added back and is meaningfully higher in sugar and NSC. If you are managing a horse with any metabolic condition, this distinction is important — always ask the feed manufacturer for the tested sugar and NSC values.

Cautions and Considerations

Choke risk: Dry beet pulp shreds or pellets expand significantly when wet. Horses that consume dry beet pulp rapidly can be at risk of choke (esophageal obstruction). While many horses eat dry beet pulp without problems, soaking beet pulp in water before feeding is a widely recommended precaution, particularly for horses with a history of choke, dental problems, or rapid eating habits. There is no nutritional disadvantage to soaking.

Calcium-phosphorus ratio: As noted above, beet pulp is high in calcium relative to phosphorus. When fed in large amounts as a hay replacer, this should be factored into the total diet calcium-phosphorus balance. This is primarily a concern when beet pulp makes up 50% or more of the forage portion of the diet.

Molasses-coated beet pulp: Not appropriate for horses with insulin resistance, EMS, Cushing's, or laminitis history. Always verify whether the beet pulp in a commercial feed is plain or contains added molasses.

Introduction: As with any feed change, beet pulp should be introduced gradually over 7–14 days to allow the hindgut microbial population to adjust.

Which Horses Benefit Most

  • Horses with insulin resistance or EMS — low glycemic impact makes beet pulp one of the preferred energy sources
  • Horses with laminitis history — fiber-based energy without the sugar and starch risk
  • Senior horses — easy to chew when soaked, provides digestible energy, supports hindgut health
  • Hard keepers — adds calories without excessive grain
  • Horses with hindgut issues — supports a stable microbial environment
  • Horses at risk of impaction colic — soaked beet pulp increases water intake

Related Ingredients

Beet pulp is often used alongside soybean hulls and alfalfa meal as part of the fiber base in low-starch feeds. It may also appear alongside molasses — when it does, check whether the molasses is coating the beet pulp itself or is a separate ingredient listed independently.
