Salt (Sodium Chloride)

An essential electrolyte that drives water intake, supports muscle and nerve function, and must be continuously replaced in sweating horses — the salt in a bag of feed is almost never enough on its own.

Plain-English Summary

Salt is sodium chloride (NaCl) — one of the most fundamental minerals in horse nutrition. Sodium is the primary electrolyte in extracellular fluid and is essential for muscle contraction, nerve impulse transmission, and maintaining blood pressure and fluid balance. Horses lose significant sodium and chloride through sweat, and sweat losses can dramatically exceed what a commercial feed provides. The presence of salt in a feed label is necessary and appropriate, but it does not eliminate the need for free-choice salt access — particularly for horses in any level of work, horses in hot climates, or horses that sweat regularly. A horse's voluntary salt consumption self-regulates to meet its needs in a way that a fixed amount in feed cannot.

What Salt Is and Why Horses Need It

Sodium chloride (common salt) provides two essential electrolytes: sodium (Na⁺) and chloride (Cl⁻). Both are critical for life.

Sodium is the primary cation (positively charged ion) in extracellular fluid — the fluid that surrounds cells. It maintains osmotic pressure, which determines how water is distributed between the bloodstream, tissues, and cells. Sodium is also essential for the sodium-potassium pump, the mechanism by which nerve cells generate electrical impulses and muscle cells contract and relax. Without adequate sodium, muscles cannot function properly and nerve transmission is impaired.

Chloride is the primary anion (negatively charged ion) in extracellular fluid and works alongside sodium to maintain fluid balance. Chloride is also a component of hydrochloric acid produced in the stomach for digestion.

Unlike most other minerals, which the horse's body can store and draw upon, sodium and chloride are rapidly excreted in urine and sweat when intake exceeds needs, and must be continuously replenished from the diet. Horses cannot effectively "save up" on sodium.

How Salt Affects Your Horse

Sweat and electrolyte loss: Horse sweat contains significant sodium and chloride, along with potassium. A horse in moderate work may lose 5–10 liters of sweat per hour in hot weather. Even at moderate sweat rates, sodium and chloride losses over a day of work can substantially exceed the amount provided in a typical commercial feed. Horses competing in endurance events, working in heat, or in sustained training need electrolyte replacement beyond what feed alone provides.

Water intake regulation: Sodium is the primary driver of thirst in horses (and in mammals generally). When blood sodium concentration rises — whether from sweat losses, salt intake, or inadequate water intake — the thirst mechanism is activated and the horse seeks water. This relationship between salt intake and water consumption is one reason that adequate salt intake is important for maintaining good hydration status. Horses with low salt intake may consume less water than they need for optimal hydration, increasing the risk of impaction colic.

Muscle function: Sodium and chloride deficiency in horses presents as weakness, reduced exercise tolerance, muscle cramping, and in severe cases, tying-up (exertional rhabdomyolysis). These signs reflect impaired muscle membrane function when electrolyte balance is disrupted. Even mild sodium deficiency can subtly impair performance and recovery in working horses.

Free-choice vs fixed dietary salt: Commercial horse feeds are formulated to provide sodium and chloride at levels adequate for horses at maintenance or light work. The guaranteed analysis lists salt as both a minimum and maximum — typically in the range of 0.3–1.0% of the feed. At a feeding rate of 4–6 lbs per day, this provides a limited total sodium intake.

A horse in moderate to heavy work, or one sweating regularly, needs more salt than a fixed feed amount provides. This is why free-choice salt access — a loose salt container or salt block in the stall and/or pasture — is the standard recommendation for all horses. With free-choice access, horses self-regulate their salt intake based on physiological need, consuming more when sweating heavily and less when at rest.

Salt blocks vs loose salt: Both forms work, but horses may not lick salt blocks as readily as they consume loose salt — particularly when sodium needs are very high. Many equine nutritionists recommend loose salt over blocks, especially for horses in work, because consumption is less restricted by the physical effort of licking. For horses with high electrolyte needs (endurance horses, horses in heavy work in heat), electrolyte supplements specifically formulated for horses provide a better-calibrated electrolyte profile than salt alone, as they also replace potassium and may include other electrolytes lost in sweat.

Reading Salt on a Feed Label

Salt is listed on the guaranteed analysis with both a minimum and maximum percentage. This is unusual — most nutrients are listed only as minimums. The reason is that salt intake is tightly regulated; too little and horses become deficient, too much can affect palatability and potentially cause problems. The min-max specification ensures the feed provides adequate sodium without excessive salt content.

Salt also appears in the ingredient list. "Salt" or "sodium chloride" appearing early in the ingredient list indicates it is present in meaningful amounts. Its position reflects the quantity used, not just a trace seasoning.

Cautions

Always provide free-choice salt access regardless of what the feed contains. Horses with kidney disease may need sodium restriction — consult your vet. Fresh water must always be available alongside any salt source — sodium without water access causes serious harm.
