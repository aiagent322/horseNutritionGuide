Fat-Soluble Vitamin / Antioxidant

Vitamin E

The most important fat-soluble antioxidant for horses — and one of the most commonly deficient nutrients in stabled or hay-fed horses.

Plain-English Summary

Vitamin E is a fat-soluble antioxidant that protects muscle cells, nerve tissue, and the immune system from oxidative damage. Fresh green pasture grass is the richest natural source — horses evolved eating it continuously. When horses are kept primarily on hay and concentrates, Vitamin E intake drops significantly because the vitamin degrades rapidly once forage is cut and dried. Horses without regular pasture access are at real risk of Vitamin E deficiency, which can cause muscle disease, neurological problems, and immune dysfunction. The form of Vitamin E in feed matters: natural Vitamin E (d-alpha-tocopherol) is substantially more bioavailable than synthetic Vitamin E (dl-alpha-tocopherol). Both appear on feed labels, and the difference is meaningful for horses with high Vitamin E needs.

What Vitamin E Is

Vitamin E is not a single compound but a family of eight related molecules — four tocopherols and four tocotrienols — of which alpha-tocopherol is the most biologically active form in horses. When a horse feed label lists "Vitamin E" or "Vitamin E supplement," it is referring to an alpha-tocopherol source.

The critical distinction between forms appears in the chemical designation: d-alpha-tocopherol (natural) versus dl-alpha-tocopherol (synthetic). The "d" and "dl" prefixes refer to the stereochemical configuration of the molecule. Natural Vitamin E (d-alpha-tocopherol) has a single stereoisomeric configuration that the horse's body recognizes and preferentially retains. Synthetic Vitamin E (dl-alpha-tocopherol) is a mixture of eight stereoisomers, only one of which is the natural form. Research in horses and other species has consistently shown that natural Vitamin E is 2–3 times more bioavailable than synthetic Vitamin E — meaning the horse must consume significantly more IU of synthetic Vitamin E to achieve the same tissue concentration.

On a feed label, look for: d-alpha tocopheryl acetate or d-alpha tocopherol (natural) versus dl-alpha tocopheryl acetate or dl-alpha tocopherol (synthetic). A generic listing of "Vitamin E supplement" does not specify the form — ask the manufacturer.

How Vitamin E Affects Your Horse

Antioxidant protection during exercise: During aerobic exercise, horses produce free radicals and reactive oxygen species as byproducts of energy metabolism. These molecules can damage cell membranes, proteins, and DNA if not neutralized. Vitamin E is the primary fat-soluble antioxidant protecting cell membranes from this oxidative damage. Horses in active training have higher Vitamin E needs than idle horses — the oxidative stress of exercise increases requirements substantially.

Muscle health: Vitamin E deficiency is associated with a condition called equine motor neuron disease (EMND) — a progressive neurodegenerative disorder affecting the motor neurons that control skeletal muscle. Horses with EMND show symmetrical muscle wasting, a low head carriage, shifting weight between hind feet, and an abnormal stance. EMND has been directly linked to prolonged Vitamin E deficiency in horses without pasture access. Supplementation can halt progression and improve clinical signs if started early, but severe cases may not fully recover.

Vitamin E also plays a role in the health of horses with equine degenerative myeloencephalopathy (EDM) — a neurological condition primarily seen in young horses that causes incoordination. While the cause of EDM is multifactorial, Vitamin E deficiency during the developmental period is considered a contributing factor.

Immune function: Vitamin E supports the proliferation and function of T-lymphocytes, the white blood cells central to the adaptive immune response. Horses with adequate Vitamin E status generally show better immune responses to vaccination and better resistance to certain infectious diseases. This effect is particularly relevant in young foals, whose immune systems are still maturing.

Reproductive health: Mares with adequate Vitamin E status during late pregnancy provide better immune protection to their foals through colostrum. Foals are born with very limited fat-soluble vitamin stores and depend on colostrum and early milk for initial Vitamin E supply.

The pasture effect: Fresh grass contains abundant alpha-tocopherol. Horses that graze green pasture for several hours a day can meet most or all of their Vitamin E needs from forage alone. The problem is that alpha-tocopherol is unstable — it degrades rapidly when grass is cut, dried, and stored as hay. By the time hay reaches a horse's feed tub, Vitamin E content may be reduced to 10–20% of fresh grass levels or less. This is why Vitamin E deficiency is far more common in stabled horses fed primarily hay than in horses with pasture access.

How Much Does Your Horse Need?

The NRC (2007) recommends 1 IU of Vitamin E per kilogram of body weight per day as a minimum for horses at maintenance — approximately 500 IU per day for a 500 kg horse. This is considered a minimum requirement to prevent deficiency, not an optimal amount.

Many equine nutritionists recommend higher amounts for:

  • Performance horses in active training: 2–4 IU/kg body weight per day (1,000–2,000 IU for a 500 kg horse)
  • Horses without pasture access: 1–2 IU/kg body weight per day at minimum
  • Horses with EMND or other neuromuscular conditions: Under veterinary guidance, often 5,000–10,000 IU per day of natural Vitamin E
  • Pregnant and lactating mares: Increased requirements, particularly in late gestation
  • Foals: High needs relative to body weight during the first weeks of life

When evaluating a commercial feed's Vitamin E contribution, multiply the IU/lb value on the label by the daily feeding rate in pounds to estimate daily intake from that feed alone. Add contributions from other feeds and supplements, then compare to your horse's estimated needs based on body weight and use.

Reading Vitamin E on a Feed Label

Vitamin E is listed on the guaranteed analysis as IU/lb or IU/kg (minimum). To evaluate whether a feed provides adequate Vitamin E for your horse at the recommended feeding rate, calculate: IU/lb × daily feeding rate in lbs = daily IU from that feed. Compare to your horse's estimated requirement based on body weight.

A feed listing 100 IU/lb fed at 4 lbs per day provides 400 IU — this may be adequate as a supplement to pasture grass but insufficient for a stabled horse in training. A feed listing 500 IU/lb fed at 4 lbs provides 2,000 IU — generous for most horses at maintenance.

The form is listed in the ingredient section, not the guaranteed analysis. Look for d-alpha (natural) vs dl-alpha (synthetic) as described above.

Cautions and Considerations

Vitamin E toxicity is rare — unlike Vitamin A and Vitamin D, Vitamin E is relatively non-toxic even at high doses. Horses have received thousands of IU per day without adverse effects in research settings. However, extremely high chronic supplementation (well above 10,000 IU/day) has not been fully studied in horses and is not recommended without veterinary guidance.

Interaction with Vitamin K: Very high doses of Vitamin E (as a tocopherol form) may interfere with Vitamin K-dependent clotting factors in some species. This is not well-established in horses but is worth noting in horses receiving anticoagulant medications.

Storage and stability: Vitamin E degrades with exposure to heat, light, and oxygen. Commercial feeds are typically formulated with Vitamin E acetate (tocopheryl acetate), which is more stable during feed processing and storage than free tocopherol. Check the feed's "best by" date — stale feed may have significantly reduced Vitamin E activity.

Interaction with selenium: Vitamin E and selenium work synergistically as antioxidants. A horse deficient in both is at greater risk of muscle damage than one deficient in either alone. Ensure both nutrients are adequate when managing horses with muscle conditions or high antioxidant demands.

Educational content only. Horses showing signs of muscle weakness, neurological symptoms, or poor performance should be evaluated by a veterinarian. Vitamin E supplementation for horses with EMND or EDM should be discussed with a veterinarian and equine nutritionist.
