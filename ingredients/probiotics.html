Digestive Support — Probiotic

Probiotics (Lactobacillus, Enterococcus, Pediococcus)

Live beneficial bacteria added to feeds to support hindgut microbial health — effective when viable, but viability through feed processing is the central question to ask about any probiotic feed.

Plain-English Summary

Probiotics are live microorganisms that, when consumed in adequate amounts, provide a health benefit to the host. In horse feeds, the most commonly used probiotic bacteria are species of Lactobacillus, Enterococcus, and Pediococcus — genera of lactic acid bacteria that naturally inhabit the horse's digestive tract. Their intended function is to support the hindgut microbial community during periods of stress or disruption. The critical issue with probiotics in commercial feeds is viability: live bacteria are sensitive to the heat, pressure, and moisture of pelleting and extrusion, and significant portions of the live organisms may be killed during feed manufacturing. This does not mean probiotic-containing feeds are without value — but the actual number of viable organisms reaching the horse's hindgut may be substantially lower than the label suggests. For horses with serious digestive concerns, a separate probiotic supplement (added at feeding time) may provide more consistent viable organism delivery than a probiotic built into a pelleted feed.

What Probiotics Are

Probiotics in the context of animal feeds are defined microorganism preparations that are intended to beneficially affect the host by improving the balance of the intestinal microbial community. The key word is "live" — probiotics must contain viable, living organisms in sufficient numbers to exert their intended effects. Dead or inactivated bacteria do not function as probiotics, though they may have some residual immunological effects from their cell wall components.

Common equine probiotic bacteria and their roles:

Lactobacillus acidophilus, L. brevis, L. plantarum — lactic acid bacteria that inhabit the foregut and small intestine. They produce lactic acid and other compounds that create an inhospitable environment for pathogenic bacteria, support epithelial integrity, and modulate immune signaling.

Enterococcus faecium — a hardy lactic acid bacterium that tolerates a wider range of pH and temperature conditions than Lactobacillus species, making it somewhat more likely to survive feed processing and the acid environment of the stomach.

Pediococcus acidilactici — a lactic acid bacterium used in feeds for its stability and its production of bacteriocins (antimicrobial compounds that inhibit certain pathogens).

Probiotic bacteria are listed on feed labels by species name. Colony Forming Units (CFU) — the measure of viable organism count — may be listed on the guaranteed analysis as CFU/gram or CFU/lb minimum.

How Probiotics Affect Your Horse

Hindgut microbial support: The cecum and large colon of the horse contain a dense and diverse microbial community essential for fiber fermentation, vitamin B synthesis, and immune function. This community can be disrupted by stress, antibiotic treatment, dietary changes, illness, or intensive exercise. Probiotic bacteria, when viable, may help stabilize and restore this community by competing with pathogenic bacteria, producing compounds that support epithelial barrier function, and stimulating immune responses that maintain microbial balance.

Stress and travel: The hindgut microbiome is particularly sensitive to stress. Research in horses has shown that transportation, competition, and intensive training are associated with measurable changes in microbial community composition. Probiotic supplementation during these periods is a rational supportive strategy, though evidence for specific outcomes in horses remains limited compared to the human and livestock probiotic literature.

Antibiotic recovery: Antibiotic treatment — particularly broad-spectrum oral antibiotics — can dramatically disrupt the hindgut microbial community, killing beneficial bacteria alongside pathogens. Probiotic supplementation during and after antibiotic treatment is a common clinical recommendation to support microbial recovery. In this context, probiotic pastes or powders added at feeding time (rather than in a pelleted feed where viability may be reduced) provide more consistent delivery of viable organisms.

Viability through processing: This is the most important practical question for probiotics in commercial feeds. Pelleting typically involves temperatures of 70–90°C (160–195°F) and significant mechanical pressure. Extrusion involves temperatures exceeding 100°C. Most lactic acid bacteria are killed at these temperatures and pressures. Enterococcus faecium is somewhat more heat-tolerant than most Lactobacillus species, which is why it is commonly selected for inclusion in pelleted feeds. Some manufacturers add probiotics post-pelleting (after the pellet has cooled) to preserve viability — this is the most reliable approach. Ask the manufacturer whether probiotics are pre- or post-pellet application if probiotic viability is important to you.

CFU counts: A meaningful probiotic effect requires a sufficient number of viable organisms reaching the target site (the hindgut). The minimum effective dose for horses is not precisely established, but research in other species suggests that billions (10⁹) of CFU per day are needed for reliable effects. Many commercial feeds contain probiotic CFU counts that, after processing losses and transit through the stomach, may not reliably deliver this level. Standalone probiotic products specifically formulated for equine use and designed for stability often provide higher and more reliable CFU delivery.

Cautions and Considerations

Probiotics are safe for horses at normal supplementation levels. They are not appropriate substitutes for veterinary treatment of serious digestive disease. For horses with compromised immune systems, discuss probiotic use with your veterinarian before supplementing. Always evaluate viability claims critically when choosing between a probiotic-containing feed and a standalone probiotic product.
