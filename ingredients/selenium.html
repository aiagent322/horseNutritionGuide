Trace Mineral — Narrow Safe Range

Selenium

An essential trace mineral with the narrowest margin between deficiency and toxicity of any nutrient in equine nutrition. Getting this right matters.

Plain-English Summary

Selenium is an essential trace mineral that horses cannot live without — but it can also kill them if they get too much. The difference between a deficient amount and a toxic amount is smaller for selenium than for almost any other nutrient. Selenium deficiency causes white muscle disease, poor immune function, and reproductive failure. Selenium toxicity causes hair and hoof loss, lameness, and in severe cases death. Soil selenium levels vary dramatically by region, meaning the same feed can be appropriate in one location and dangerous in another when combined with local hay. This is why total daily selenium from all sources — feed, hay, water, and supplements — must be evaluated together, not in isolation.

What Selenium Is and Why Horses Need It

Selenium is a nonmetallic trace element that functions primarily as an antioxidant in the horse's body. It is a structural component of a family of enzymes called selenoproteins, the most important of which for horses is glutathione peroxidase — an enzyme that protects cells from oxidative damage by neutralizing harmful peroxides produced during normal metabolism and exercise.

Selenium works closely with Vitamin E in this antioxidant role. The two nutrients are partially interchangeable in some protective functions, but they are not fully substitutable — a horse deficient in both selenium and Vitamin E is at significantly greater risk of muscle damage than a horse deficient in only one.

Beyond antioxidant function, selenium plays roles in thyroid hormone metabolism, immune system function, and reproductive health. Mares with inadequate selenium status have been associated with retained placenta and reduced reproductive performance in some studies, though this relationship is complex and influenced by many factors.

How Selenium Affects Your Horse

When selenium is deficient: The most recognized consequence of selenium deficiency in horses is nutritional myodegeneration, commonly called white muscle disease. This condition causes degeneration of skeletal and cardiac muscle, presenting as stiffness, weakness, difficulty swallowing, and in foals, an inability to nurse or stand. The name comes from the pale, whitish appearance of affected muscle tissue at necropsy. White muscle disease is most commonly seen in foals born to selenium-deficient mares, but can occur in horses of any age on selenium-poor diets.

Selenium deficiency in adult horses often presents more subtly — reduced exercise tolerance, poor recovery from work, a dull coat, impaired immune function, and susceptibility to infection. These signs are nonspecific and can be caused by many conditions, which is why blood selenium testing (or whole blood selenoprotein activity testing) is the appropriate way to confirm deficiency.

When selenium is adequate: Horses with good selenium status show better antioxidant capacity, particularly during and after intense exercise when oxidative stress is highest. Well-conditioned performance horses, horses in heavy training, and horses under the physiological stress of pregnancy or lactation have higher selenium needs than idle horses.

When selenium is excessive: Selenium toxicity (selenosis) is a serious and potentially fatal condition. Chronic toxicity — the most common form — develops when horses consume moderately elevated selenium over weeks or months. The hallmark signs are loss of long mane and tail hair, horizontal cracking and separation of the hoof wall at the coronary band, and general lameness. Horses may also show weight loss, dullness, and impaired liver function. Acute toxicity from a single massive dose causes neurological signs, respiratory distress, and can be fatal. Selenium toxicity is not reversible once tissue damage has occurred — prevention is the only effective approach.

The critical concept is that selenium toxicity can result from the accumulation of selenium from multiple sources that are each, individually, within safe limits. A horse eating a commercial feed at 0.3 ppm selenium, hay from a high-selenium region, and a supplement containing selenium can easily exceed safe total intake without any single source being obviously dangerous.

Safe Range and Recommended Intake

The National Research Council (NRC) Nutrient Requirements of Horses (2007) suggests a dietary selenium concentration of 0.1 mg/kg dry matter as adequate for maintenance, with a recommended dietary allowance of 0.1–0.3 mg/kg for most horses. The NRC's maximum tolerable level is 2.0 mg/kg in the total diet.

In practical terms, most equine nutritionists target a total daily selenium intake of approximately 1–3 mg per day for a 500 kg (1,100 lb) horse at maintenance, with higher amounts for horses in heavy work or during pregnancy and lactation. The absolute upper limit from all sources combined is generally considered to be approximately 5 mg/day for a mature horse, though chronic intake above 2 mg/day from all sources warrants monitoring.

Regional hay selenium levels in the United States vary from severely deficient (parts of the Pacific Northwest and Northeast, where soils are selenium-poor) to potentially toxic (parts of the Great Plains, particularly South Dakota, Wyoming, and Nebraska, where selenium-accumulating plants grow). This geographic variation is one reason that selenium cannot be evaluated in isolation from the total diet.

Organic vs Inorganic Selenium: What the Difference Means for Your Horse

Selenium in horse feed appears in two primary forms: inorganic (sodium selenite or sodium selenate) and organic (selenium yeast, selenium proteinate, or selenomethionine).

Sodium selenite is the most common inorganic form and has been used in commercial feeds for decades. It is absorbed in the small intestine and used directly by the body for selenoprotein synthesis. It is effective at preventing deficiency but is not stored in muscle tissue to any significant degree.

Selenium yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae enriched with organic selenium) provides selenium primarily as selenomethionine — an organic form that is incorporated into muscle protein in place of methionine. This means selenium yeast builds a larger selenium reserve in muscle tissue, which can be drawn upon during periods of high demand. Research in horses has generally shown that selenium yeast provides superior tissue selenium retention compared to sodium selenite at equivalent dietary concentrations.

For most horses on adequate diets, the practical difference between forms is modest. For horses with high selenium needs (performance horses, mares in late pregnancy, horses recovering from myopathies), the tissue storage advantage of organic selenium may be clinically meaningful. The higher bioavailability of organic forms also means that the same blood selenium level can be achieved at a lower total intake — a relevant consideration when managing total selenium load.

Some commercial feeds contain both forms — organic selenium for tissue storage and sodium selenite for immediate metabolic availability. This dual-source approach is common in well-formulated performance feeds.

Reading Selenium on a Feed Label

Selenium is listed on the guaranteed analysis panel as a minimum in mg/kg or ppm (parts per million). These two units are equivalent: 1 mg/kg = 1 ppm.

The selenium source(s) appear in the ingredient list. Look for: selenium yeast, organic selenium, selenomethionine, selenium proteinate (organic sources) or sodium selenite, sodium selenate (inorganic sources).

To estimate daily selenium intake from a feed, multiply the ppm value by the daily feeding rate in kilograms (lbs ÷ 2.2 = kg): a feed with 0.3 ppm selenium fed at 3 kg/day provides approximately 0.9 mg selenium from that feed. Add hay and supplement contributions to get the total.

If the guaranteed analysis does not list selenium, ask the manufacturer — it should always be disclosed on a complete commercial feed.

Cautions and Considerations

  • Always evaluate total daily intake from all sources — feed, hay, water, and supplements together.
  • Know your region — if you live in a high-selenium area, your hay may already be providing near-adequate selenium even without supplementation.
  • Avoid stacking selenium supplements — if the feed contains selenium and you also give a supplement or ration balancer with selenium, calculate the combined daily dose.
  • Test your hay — hay analysis from a laboratory (Equi-Analytical and similar services) will include selenium. This is the only way to know what your hay is contributing.
  • Blood testing — whole blood selenium or plasma glutathione peroxidase activity testing can confirm your horse's selenium status. Discuss with your veterinarian if you have concerns about deficiency or toxicity.
Educational content only. Selenium management in horses should involve your veterinarian, particularly if you suspect deficiency or toxicity, or if your horse is a foal, pregnant mare, or performance horse with high demands.
