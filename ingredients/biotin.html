B Vitamin — Hoof & Coat Support

Biotin

The best-researched nutritional intervention for poor hoof quality in horses — with real evidence behind it, real timelines, and real expectations.

Plain-English Summary

Biotin is a water-soluble B vitamin (Vitamin B7) that plays an essential role in the synthesis of keratin — the structural protein that makes up hoof horn, hair, and skin. Research conducted primarily in Europe over several decades has demonstrated that supplementing horses with adequate biotin at meaningful doses can improve hoof horn quality in horses with poor feet. The key word is "adequate" — the doses used in research (15–20 mg per day) are substantially higher than the trace amounts found in most commercial feeds. Results take time: because biotin affects only new hoof growth and horses grow approximately 0.25–0.4 inches of hoof per month, it takes 6–12 months before improved horn quality becomes visible at the ground surface. Biotin does not repair existing hoof damage — it improves the quality of hoof horn as it grows.

What Biotin Is

Biotin is a water-soluble vitamin in the B-complex family, also known as Vitamin B7 or Vitamin H. Like other B vitamins, it functions as a coenzyme — a molecule that activates specific enzymes in the body's metabolic pathways. Biotin specifically activates a group of enzymes called carboxylases, which are involved in fatty acid synthesis, gluconeogenesis (glucose production), and amino acid metabolism.

For hoof health, the most relevant of biotin's roles is its involvement in the synthesis of keratin — the fibrous structural protein that forms the hoof wall, sole, frog, and white line of the horse's hoof. Biotin is required for the normal differentiation of the keratinocytes (horn-producing cells) in the coronary band and hoof wall. When biotin is insufficient, keratinocyte function is impaired and the resulting hoof horn may be softer, more brittle, and more prone to cracking and chipping.

Horses can synthesize some biotin through microbial fermentation in the hindgut. However, the extent to which hindgut-synthesized biotin is absorbed and available to the horse is not fully established, and dietary biotin is considered the more reliable source for horses with poor hoof quality.

How Biotin Affects Your Horse

On hoof quality: The evidence for biotin's effect on hoof quality is more robust than for most other equine nutritional supplements. Multiple controlled studies, primarily conducted in Europe, have demonstrated improvements in hoof horn hardness, tensile strength, and the frequency of hoof wall defects (cracks, white line disease susceptibility, and wall separation) in horses supplemented with biotin at therapeutic doses.

The mechanism is direct: biotin supports the normal differentiation and function of horn-producing cells (keratinocytes) in the coronary band. Better-functioning keratinocytes produce harder, more tightly packed hoof horn tubules. The result, over time, is a hoof wall with better structural integrity.

Importantly, this effect is most pronounced in horses that begin with poor hoof quality. Horses with naturally good, hard feet may show less dramatic improvement because their baseline keratinocyte function is already adequate. Biotin is not a universal improvement agent — it corrects a deficit rather than enhancing already-normal hoof horn production.

On skin and coat: Biotin is involved in the synthesis of keratin throughout the body, not just in the hoof. Horses receiving adequate biotin often show improvement in coat quality — improved shine, reduced brittleness of the mane and tail, and better skin condition. These effects are secondary to the hoof benefits in research attention but are real and commonly observed by horse owners.

The timeline reality: The hoof wall grows at approximately 0.25–0.4 inches per month at the coronary band, with full hoof replacement taking 9–12 months in most horses. Biotin affects only new horn produced from the coronary band downward — existing horn is not improved. This means visible improvement at the ground surface (where shoes contact the hoof) takes the longest to appear. Many horse owners and farriers observe the first meaningful changes at 6 months and more complete improvement at 9–12 months. If no improvement is seen after 12 months of consistent supplementation at adequate doses, other causes of poor hoof quality should be investigated.

Other factors in hoof quality: It is important to recognize that biotin deficiency is not the only — or even the most common — cause of poor hoof quality in horses. Genetics, hydration-dehydration cycles (wet-dry conditions), footing type, farriery frequency and quality, diet balance (particularly zinc, copper, and methionine), and systemic health all influence hoof quality. Biotin addresses one piece of the puzzle. A horse with poor feet on an otherwise nutritionally complete diet in good management may benefit substantially from biotin. A horse with poor feet due to a wet/dry environment, infrequent farriery, or genetic predisposition will see limited benefit from biotin supplementation alone.

How Much Biotin Does Your Horse Need?

This is where the gap between commercial feed biotin levels and therapeutic biotin levels becomes important. The doses used in controlled research on equine hoof quality have generally ranged from 15 to 20 mg of biotin per day. This is the amount supported by evidence as potentially effective for horses with poor hoof quality.

Most commercial horse feeds contain biotin in amounts ranging from 0.1 to 0.5 mg per lb. A horse consuming 4–6 lbs of a typical commercial feed daily receives approximately 0.4–3 mg of biotin from that feed. This is well below the 15–20 mg threshold associated with therapeutic effects on hoof quality.

Dedicated hoof supplements and high-biotin feeds specifically formulated for hoof support typically contain 15–30 mg of biotin per daily serving. These are the products that have the best chance of producing the hoof improvements described in research.

When evaluating a commercial feed's biotin content for a horse with hoof quality concerns, check the mg per lb value and calculate daily intake at the recommended feeding rate. If the total falls substantially short of 15 mg per day, a dedicated biotin supplement may be warranted — discuss with your vet or equine nutritionist.

Reading Biotin on a Feed Label

Biotin appears on the guaranteed analysis as mg/lb or mg/kg (minimum). It may also appear in the ingredient list as: biotin, d-biotin, or less commonly Vitamin H.

A feed listing 0.34 mg/lb of biotin fed at 4 lbs per day provides approximately 1.36 mg of biotin daily — far below the 15–20 mg research threshold for hoof quality effects. A feed specifically formulated for hoof support might list 5 mg/lb or more, providing 20+ mg per day at 4 lbs.

When a feed is marketed with hoof support claims, check the actual mg/lb value on the guaranteed analysis and calculate the daily dose at the recommended feeding rate before concluding whether it is likely to be effective for your horse's situation.

Cautions and Considerations

Biotin is extremely safe — it is water-soluble, and excess is excreted in urine. There is no established toxic dose for biotin in horses, and supplementation well above the research therapeutic range has not produced adverse effects in published studies.

Biotin interference with laboratory tests: In humans, high-dose biotin supplementation is known to interfere with certain immunoassay laboratory tests, causing falsely elevated or falsely decreased results for thyroid hormones, troponin, and other markers. Whether similar interference occurs in equine laboratory testing is not well-established, but if your horse is having blood work done that includes immunoassays, it is worth informing your veterinarian that the horse is receiving high-dose biotin supplementation.

Not a substitute for balanced nutrition: Biotin works best as part of a nutritionally complete diet. Zinc, copper, methionine, and adequate protein are all necessary for normal hoof horn synthesis. A horse deficient in these nutrients will not achieve optimal hoof quality from biotin alone. The amino acid methionine is particularly relevant — it is required for keratin synthesis alongside biotin.

Consistency matters: Biotin supplementation needs to be maintained consistently to see ongoing benefit. Stopping supplementation will, over time, result in the return of previous hoof quality as old horn grows out and is replaced by horn produced without biotin support.

Related Nutrients for Hoof Health

For comprehensive hoof support, biotin is most effective when combined with adequate levels of zinc, copper, and the amino acid methionine. These nutrients collectively support keratin synthesis, cross-linking of hoof horn proteins, and the structural integrity of the hoof wall. A horse receiving excellent biotin supplementation but deficient in zinc or copper may show limited hoof improvement.

Educational content only. Persistent poor hoof quality can have many causes beyond nutrition, including genetics, environment, farriery, and systemic health conditions. A veterinarian and an experienced farrier should both be involved in assessing a horse with significantly compromised hoof integrity.
